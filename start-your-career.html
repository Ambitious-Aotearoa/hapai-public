Start your career

Are you thinking about a career as a public servant? Our guide to public service can help you gain clarity over your career path.

What is a public servant?

In Aotearoa New Zealand, a public servant is any person who works for central or local government.

Public servants work all around Aotearoa New Zealand. About one in five jobs are in the public sector.

A skill unique to public servants is writing policy and so shaping the future of our country. But that’s not all public servants do! They work across a wide range of fields from health to justice, immigration to education, border security to conservation.

Is public service a career path?

Yes, absolutely. Many people are drawn to being a public servant because they want to make a difference and contribute to creating a better New Zealand.

About 1 in 5 jobs in Aotearoa New Zealand are in the public sector. From working in customer-facing roles to developing policy, a career in the public sector can be exciting, challenging and rewarding.

There are many ways to navigate your public service career – whether you move between government agencies, across the public-private-NFP sectors, or gain experience overseas – the public sector needs diversity in its people and their experience.

No matter what part of the public sector you hope to work in, engaging with Hāpai Public can help.

We will help you deepen your understanding of the public sector, connect with other professionals and learn about future opportunities. You don’t need to be working in the public sector to join. Hāpai Public supports those from outside the public sector who are engaged in or interested in public service.

How do I begin my career?

If you are interested in beginning a career in the public sector, there are many ways to start. One of the first steps you can take is to define what area of the public sector you would like to explore. Consider whether you want to work in front-line services or take a more administration-based approach.

Are you attracted to a particular area of work, like conservation, policing or education?

The public sector spans many roles, disciplines, agencies and professions. What binds them all together is the desire to work in service of the public and improve outcomes for all New Zealanders. Exploring the website of the Public Service Commission Te Kawa Mataaho is a good next step.

What role is right for me?

The New Zealand Public Sector has room for people with a wide range of strengths and skills.

From caring for our environment, protecting our people and tackling complex issues, every role is important.

Explore some examples of roles in the public services

Central Government

The central government is made up of departments, departmental agencies and crown agencies. Core executive agencies deliver policy advice, regulation, government services, funding and commissioning.

To work for the central government is to support the constitutional and democratic process and deliver efficient public services, from providing customer support for Inland Revenue to court bailiff's for the Ministry of Justice.

Emergency Services

Emergency services play a vital role in helping keep New Zealanders safe. As part of our emergency services — fire, police and ambulance — job roles include working on the front line as a first responder or provide support through back office administrative services.

Emergency services job roles include police officers, paramedics, firefighters, emergency dispatchers, managers, and communications personnel.

Local Government

When people think of the Public Sector they often think of working for the national government.

Local government is an important part of public service. Working for a city, district or regional council means implementing decisions that support local people. From resource management to the management of local parks and gardens, a career in local government means looking after the things that make your local community tick.

Work in Central Government as a Policy Advisor

A policy analyst or policy advisor assists in the development of government policies. They use research to provide neutral advice to help the government of the day implement their policies. In your role, you may identify any issues that need to be reported on, develop, interpret and review existing legislation and provide advice and recommendations to your minister to help them make policy decisions. Policy analysts usually have a bachelors degree with many holding a postgraduate qualification.

Work in Emergency Services as a Police Officer

Police officers work to make our communities safer. They do this by responding to emergencies, investigating and solving crimes, keeping the peace, and providing safety and crime prevention education to youth.

New police officers are required to undergo 20 weeks of training at the Royal New Zealand Police College before they can work as a police officer. Other pathways from completing training include working in over 30 areas of policing from joining a specialist squad such as the Armed Offenders Squad to investigation roles like the Financial Crime Group.

Work in Local Government as an Emergency Management Advisor

Emergency management. advisors develop civil defense plans that keep their local communities safe during emergencies. As part of their role they may develop solutions for preventing floods and responding to other natural disasters like earthquakes and fires. They may work on things like improving stock banks to prevent surface flooding and developing plans for keeping residents safe as they evacuate their homes and workplaces. They often liaise with police and other emergency services in preparation for a major disaster response.
